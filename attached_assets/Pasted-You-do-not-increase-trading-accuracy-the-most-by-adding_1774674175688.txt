You do **not** increase trading “accuracy” the most by adding more indicators. You increase it by making the bot **trade less, in cleaner conditions, with stricter filtering, better labels, and more honest validation**. Also, no strategy gets to reliable 100% accuracy in live markets, and simulated results can overstate performance because they do not fully capture liquidity, execution, and hindsight effects. ([CFTC][1])

For **Godsview**, the best upgrades are these:

## 1. Stop optimizing for raw win rate alone

A high win rate can still lose money if losses are larger than wins or if costs eat the edge. Futures commissions and fees are real, and even low per-contract fees matter when you trade often. ([Interactive Brokers][2])

What to optimize instead:

* expectancy per trade
* profit factor
* max drawdown
* Sharpe
* post-cost net PnL
* stability across sessions and months

In practice:

* Reject any setup that only looks good **before** fees and slippage.
* Prefer setups that remain profitable after realistic execution costs.

---

## 2. Make the strategy more selective, not more active

The fastest way to raise live accuracy is usually to **cut mediocre trades**.

Upgrade your setup logic like this:

* Only trade during your best session windows
* Only trade when spread is below a threshold
* Only trade when volatility is in a healthy range
* Only trade when regime matches the setup
* Skip trades near major news
* Skip the first weak signal after a structural break unless confirmation is strong

This is already aligned with your layered design. Your biggest accuracy gains will likely come from turning your current setup detector into a **high-conviction gate** rather than trying to invent ten new setups.

For Godsview:

* `absorption_reversal` should be allowed mainly in rotation/range conditions
* `continuation` should be allowed mainly in trend/expansion conditions
* `breakout_failure` should only trigger when extension and exhaustion are both present
* `cvd_divergence` should require structural context, not divergence alone

That kind of setup-to-regime matching is one of the highest-value upgrades you can make.

---

## 3. Upgrade labels, not just features

A lot of trading systems fail because their labels are weak.

Instead of “did price go up after entry,” use **better event-based labels**:

* triple-barrier labeling
* time stop + stop-loss + take-profit outcome labeling
* meta-labeling for “take this signal or skip it”

Meta-strategy research from López de Prado focuses on supervising and improving base strategies rather than blindly predicting direction, which is much closer to what your Claude/risk layer is trying to do. ([SSRN][3])

For Godsview, the best label upgrade is:

* **Primary model**: detect whether a setup exists
* **Meta-model**: decide whether this specific detected setup is worth taking

That single change is often more powerful than adding more indicators.

---

## 4. Split detection and execution quality

Do not ask one model to do everything.

Use separate components:

* **Setup detector**: “Is this an absorption reversal / continuation / sweep reclaim?”
* **Quality model**: “How likely is this setup to reach target before stop?”
* **Execution model**: “Should I enter now, wait one bar, or skip?”

That gives you cleaner learning and fewer conflicting patterns.

For Godsview specifically:

* Keep your setup detector rules relatively interpretable
* Train a separate model per setup
* Then add a meta-model on top of those setup-specific outputs

That is much better than one giant model trying to learn all market behaviors at once.

---

## 5. Add regime detection that is actually useful

Many systems say “trend” or “range” but do not act differently enough.

You should classify regime using:

* directional persistence
* realized volatility
* distance from VWAP/session value
* sweep frequency
* delta persistence
* failed breakout frequency
* rotation vs expansion behavior

Then use the regime in three ways:

* block bad setups
* raise thresholds in bad regimes
* change stop/target logic by regime

Example:

* In **range**, favor reclaim/reversal setups with tighter targets
* In **trend**, favor continuation and pullback setups
* In **expansion**, require strong order-flow confirmation before entering
* In **chop**, do not trade

This usually boosts live accuracy more than adding another momentum oscillator.

---

## 6. Use stricter confirmation stacks

The cleanest high-accuracy setups often need **confluence**, not a single trigger.

For each setup, require 3 layers:

* **structure**: where price is relative to key levels/session structure
* **flow**: whether aggressive participation supports the move
* **timing**: whether the entry is late, early, or well-positioned

Example for a long sweep reclaim:

* sweep below local low
* immediate reclaim back above level
* positive short-horizon delta shift
* absorption or failed follow-through on the downside
* spread still acceptable
* no news lockout

If only one or two of those are true, skip it.

That will reduce trade count, but usually improve accuracy.

---

## 7. Add “do not trade” intelligence

The biggest edge often comes from knowing when **not** to trade.

Build explicit no-trade filters:

* wide spread relative to ATR
* lunchtime liquidity
* first bar after major news
* repeated signal clustering in chop
* low conviction from flow and structure disagreement
* too many recent failed signals from the same setup
* session already exhausted after your daily edge window passed

For Godsview, add:

* **setup cooldown after two failures**
* **session kill-switch after repeated weak-quality detections**
* **dynamic no-trade state** when order flow becomes contradictory

This is one of the most practical ways to improve live hit rate.

---

## 8. Make Claude a veto specialist, not a confidence booster

Your Claude layer should mostly answer:

* Is the structure coherent?
* Is the signal too late?
* Is flow contradicting price?
* Is the setup occurring in poor session context?
* Is this likely a fake breakout / exhaustion move?

Do not let Claude “invent” edge. Let it reject trades that are statistically okay but contextually ugly.

Best upgrade:

* measure Claude’s contribution with ablation:

  * ML only
  * ML + order flow
  * ML + order flow + Claude
* keep Claude only if vetoed trades perform worse than accepted trades

That keeps the system honest.

---

## 9. Improve validation or the accuracy number is meaningless

This is one of the biggest issues in trading ML.

If you want higher **real** accuracy, you need more honest testing:

* walk-forward testing
* purged / embargoed splits for time-series leakage control
* out-of-month and out-of-regime validation
* post-cost evaluation
* separate replay and paper-trade evaluation

General cross-validation research supports careful model selection and weighting, but in trading the bigger issue is leakage and temporal dependence, so standard random splits can seriously mislead you. ([SSRN][4])

For Godsview:

* train on one block
* validate on the next block
* test on the next unseen block
* rotate forward
* compare by session, regime, and setup

If a setup only works in one month, it is not ready.

---

## 10. Calibrate thresholds per setup

Different setups should not share the same score threshold.

You should learn, per setup:

* minimum ML probability
* minimum OF score
* minimum final score
* minimum reward-to-risk quality
* allowed sessions
* allowed regimes

Example:

* `continuation` may work at 0.70 final score in strong trend
* `breakout_failure` may need 0.82 and stronger flow contradiction
* `cvd_divergence` may need very strict structural filters to avoid noise

This often improves accuracy a lot because each setup has different signal quality.

---

## 11. Add trade clustering and recency memory

Markets often repeat failure modes for a while.

Track:

* last 5 signals of same setup
* same-level repeated failures
* consecutive losses by setup
* recent false breaks in current session
* whether similar trade already failed nearby

Then reduce confidence or block the next one.

This is a very practical upgrade for Godsview’s recall layer.

---

## 12. Improve execution timing

A good setup can still be a bad entry.

Add entry policies:

* market now
* limit on micro pullback
* wait one confirming bar
* enter only if reclaim holds for N seconds / one bar
* cancel if immediate adverse flow appears

Often the edge is not in “what” to trade, but in “when exactly” to enter after the setup appears.

---

## 13. Use realistic execution costs from day one

Even “accurate” systems can fall apart after costs. Interactive Brokers’ published futures commission schedules show there are real per-contract costs, and that is before you fully account for slippage and exchange/regulatory fees. ([Interactive Brokers][2])

So in Godsview you should:

* simulate spread widening by session
* simulate worse fills in fast markets
* add partial fill or missed fill logic
* stress-test with 1x, 1.5x, and 2x baseline slippage

If the strategy only works under ideal fills, it is not accurate enough.

---

## 14. The most effective upgrades for Godsview specifically

If your goal is “highest possible real-world accuracy,” I’d prioritize these in this exact order:

**A. Better setup definitions**

* tighten each setup with structure + flow + timing conditions

**B. Per-setup meta-labeling**

* separate “signal exists” from “trade this signal”

**C. Regime-specific thresholds**

* different acceptance rules by setup and regime

**D. Strong no-trade filters**

* session, news, spread, chop, clustering

**E. Walk-forward evaluation**

* no random split optimism

**F. Execution realism**

* slippage, partial fills, bad opens, exit misses

**G. Claude veto analytics**

* keep only if it improves post-cost results

---

## What “highest accuracy” really looks like

In practice, the strongest trading systems usually look like this:

* fewer trades
* narrower conditions
* strong skip logic
* adaptive thresholds
* brutally honest validation
* stable edge across time, not one backtest window

That is how you raise **live** accuracy.
